Understaffed, an understatement

Meet Mr. Marita Aggree. He’s the nurse, pharmacists, counselor, mentor, friend, and only clinician at Family Health Option Kenya’s clinic in Kibera, the largest slum in West Africa. Kibera is sure to shock the senses as children hop over slow moving open sewage creeks while playing chase through a 2.5 square km dirt maze that is home to an estimated 250,000 of Nairobi’s 3.3 million residents.

 

“They do not get lost, this is their home.” Amos chuckles as he answers my question as to whether kids get lost in the twisting, turning alleys connecting the homes that are cleverly patched together by corrugated metal and any other scraps made useful by the resourceful residents of Kibera. Amos is the director of Family Health Options Kenya (FHOK), the largest provider of family planning services and supplies in Kenya. FHOK, with 14 clinics, provided over 2.9 million services last year alone. Amos is giving me a tour of the family planning clinic in Kibera, a structure made from 2 shipping containers with a roof connecting them. “We love the Kibera clinic,” he says, “it is our mandate to serve the most marginalized people.”

There are more children than adults in the waiting area as Mr. Aggree, Amos, and Melvine, my personal hero, show us around (more on Melvine later). Mr. Aggree starts the tour by showing us the small room  in which he sees patients. Then he walks us across the hall to where he fills prescriptions. Then he shows us the room where he does pelvic examinations and IUD insertions. Then he shows us the room where he gives children immunizations for half a dollar. Finally, we go into the laboratory where we meet the lab technician who runs the STI testing, pregnancy tests, and more. It was a relief to see another staff member. There are 4 people who work at this clinic: Mr. Aggree, the lab tech, a receptionist, and a security guard. They handle 30-40 patients a day. I was at the clinic for about 20 minutes and already the line was growing; it was time to go. Mr. Aggree smiles at his patients then looks to me, “We are very busy.” That might just be the understatement of the year.

 

The Kibera clinic costs $60,000 USD per year for adequate staff and supplies, but this funding has been slashed due to the wave of US centric policies causing devastation in the developing world. FHOK had to let go of 6 staff members at the Kibera clinic in recent months just to stay afloat. Given the oversized consumption footprint of the US and the millions who suffer in factories, farms, and elsewhere to satiate the desires of a consumerist society, I’d say that 1% of our federal budget is a low ball figure for humanitarian aid. Not to mention the fact that the US economy was built on (and would be nothing without) the 400 years of unpaid slave labor that we are still struggling to take responsibility for. We can and should play a bigger role in the international humanitarian scene. Give Mr. Aggree a break.
